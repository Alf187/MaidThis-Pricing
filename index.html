Objection Playbook

When they push back

Every objection has a path through it. Follow the steps in order and don't skip ahead. The script lines are a starting point, not a word-for-word script. Make it sound like you.

Price
They say
"That's too expensive" or "That's more than I expected"
⌄
1

Don't drop the price first. Acknowledge and anchor value.

Before you touch the number, make sure they feel heard. Then remind them what they're actually getting. Insured, background-checked cleaners who show up every time and back it with a 24-hour happiness guarantee. That's not a commodity.

"I totally get it, it's a real commitment. I want to make sure you know what's included before we look at anything else. We're fully insured, all our cleaners are background-checked, and if anything's not right we'll fix it within 24 hours, no charge. A lot of clients tell us they tried cheaper options first and ended up calling us anyway."
★ Text them our Google reviews
2

Offer the one-time new client discount: $15 off.

Only pull this out after you've defended value. Don't lead with it or it trains them to always push back on price. Frame it as something you're doing for them specifically, not a standard promo.

"What I can do for a first-time client is take $15 off the first clean. That brings you to [adjusted price] to see how it goes. If you love it, we set you up on a recurring plan at the discounted rate going forward."
3

Still no? Offer to right-size the scope with hourly.

If the full-home price isn't landing, shift to hourly and let them control the budget. Pick the zones they care most about and build a custom clean from there. Gets them in the door and builds trust.

"Another option is we can do this by the hour at $50 per hour. You tell me what you want focused on, kitchen and bathrooms, main living areas, whatever matters most. We can cover a lot in 3 or 4 hours for around $150 to $200. A lot of clients start that way and then move to a full clean once they see the quality."
4

Last move: smaller recurring frequency.

If budget is genuinely tight, drop the frequency, not the deep clean. Monthly recurring is your lowest price tier and still locks them in. Never just give up the sale.

"If the weekly or bi-weekly schedule feels like a lot right now, we can start you monthly. That's our most flexible plan and it locks in the lower recurring rate. You can always upgrade later."
Stall
They say
"I need to think about it" or "Let me look around first"
⌄
1

Find out what's actually behind it.

"Need to think about it" almost always means something specific. Price, timing, trust, or they want to check one other company. Ask directly and you'll get a real answer 80% of the time.

"Of course, I want you to feel good about it. Can I ask, is there a specific part you're unsure about? Sometimes I can clear something up right now and save you the back and forth."
2

Create a light sense of urgency without being pushy.

Our schedule fills up fast, especially on weekday mornings. You can use that truthfully. Don't invent fake scarcity, but if there's a real window, use it.

"I get it, no rush. The only thing I'll mention is our schedule fills up pretty quickly for the slots people usually want, mornings and early afternoons. I can hold a spot for you for 24 hours if you want to think it over and come back to me tomorrow. After that it usually gets taken."
3

Reduce the risk so there's nothing to think about.

The happiness guarantee is your strongest closing tool for hesitant clients. If they can get a full clean and flag anything wrong within 24 hours, there's basically no risk in trying.

"Here's the thing, there's really nothing to lose. We back every clean with a 24-hour happiness guarantee. If anything's not right, we come back and fix it at no charge. You can try one clean and see for yourself before committing to anything recurring."
4

Set a clear follow-up before you hang up.

Never end a call with "feel free to reach back out." You lose control of the sale. Lock in a specific time to follow up before you get off the phone.

"No problem at all. What if I check back in with you tomorrow afternoon, say around 2? That gives you time to think it over and I can answer any questions you have then."
Third Party
They say
"I need to run it by my husband / wife first"
⌄
1

Validate it, then find out if there's a real blocker underneath.

Sometimes this is genuine. Sometimes it's a polite way to say no without confrontation. A quick question tells you which one it is.

"That makes total sense. Quick question though, is there anything about the service itself you're unsure of, or is it mainly just a heads up to them before booking? I want to make sure you have everything you need to have that conversation."
2

Give them the tools to sell it internally.

They're going to have to sell it to their spouse for you. Make that easy. Summarize the key points in one or two sentences they can repeat: insured, guaranteed, saves them real hours every week.

"Totally fine. Here's the short version you can share with them. We're a local insured service, background-checked cleaners, and if anything's off we fix it within 24 hours at no cost. For a [home size] home it's [price] per visit on a recurring plan. A lot of families find it actually saves more than it costs when you factor in the time."
★ Text them our Google reviews
3

Try to get both decision-makers on the call now.

If you can, get the spouse on the phone in the moment. You lose a lot of deals in the handoff between spouses because the excitement doesn't transfer. This is a soft ask, not pressure.

"If your husband/wife is around right now, would it be easier to just loop them in real quick so I can answer both of your questions at once? Takes two minutes and then you've got everything you need to decide together."
4

Lock a specific follow-up time. Don't leave it open-ended.

"No worries at all, take your time. When do you think you'll have a chance to talk with them? I can follow up right after that so the timing works for everyone."
Watch for this If they say "I'll call you when we decide" without agreeing to a time, that's usually a dead end. Politely push for a window: "Sounds good, just so I can plan, would that be in the next day or two, or more like next week?"
Competitor
They say
"I already have someone who cleans for me"
⌄
1

Respect it, then ask what they have.

Don't go negative on whoever they're using. Ask what they're getting and let the comparison happen naturally. Most people with informal cleaners don't have a guarantee, insurance, or consistency.

"That's great, happy to hear you're already taken care of. Can I ask, is it a company or an individual? Just curious what setup you have."
2

Highlight what they probably don't have.

Individual cleaners are rarely insured or bonded. There's no backup if they cancel or stop showing up. That's your edge. Don't be aggressive, just factual.

"The main difference with us is we're fully insured and bonded, which protects you if anything ever gets damaged. And if your person ever can't make it, there's no gap, we always send someone. A lot of our clients came to us after their individual cleaner stopped showing up or raised their rate without warning."
★ Text them our Google reviews
3

Plant a seed for when things change.

Not every competitor situation is closeable right now. That's fine. Leave a good impression and a door open. They'll call you when their current cleaner flakes.

"Totally understood. I'll send you our info just so you have it. If anything ever changes with your current situation, we'd love to be the first call you make."
Closing Tips

Habits that help you close

Small things that separate reps who consistently close from the ones who always seem to just miss.

📞

Always ask for the booking before you hang up

Even if you've handled every objection, you still have to ask. "Do you want to go ahead and get that first clean on the calendar?" is your job on every call. Don't assume a warm conversation is a yes.

😁

Let them hear a smile through the phone

Nobody wants a call center robot cleaning their home. Smile before you pick up, use their name, sound like a neighbor. Warmth closes more deals than polish. If you sound like a script, they'll treat you like one.

🕐

Talk about time, not just money

A lot of clients don't buy cleaning, they buy back their weekends. "A lot of our clients say the biggest thing isn't the clean, it's not having to think about it" hits differently than leading with the price.

💰

Lead with the recurring rate, not the deep clean price

When you open with the recurring rate as the value, price feels lower. "Your ongoing visits would be $165" sounds better than "$275 for the first clean, then $165 after." Frame the value first.

🛠

Never negotiate against yourself

Don't volunteer a discount before they ask. Give the real price with confidence. If they push back, that's when you move through the steps. A lot of reps drop price before the client even flinches.

✅

The 24-hour guarantee is your best closing tool

When someone is on the fence, the guarantee removes their risk. "If anything's not right, we fix it within 24 hours, no questions" gives them permission to say yes without feeling locked in forever.

💬

Ask questions, don't just answer them

Good reps listen more than they pitch. "What's the biggest thing you're hoping a cleaning service takes off your plate?" tells you exactly what to sell. Use their answer in your close. A quote lands harder when it's built around what they actually said.

📱

Book the spot, don't suggest a spot

"I've got Tuesday at 10 or Thursday at 1, which works better?" beats "when would you like to schedule?" Giving two good options feels easier to say yes to than an open-ended question. Assume the close and offer the choice.
